# OxRound — Company Structure & Operations Plan

## Phase 1: The Founding Team (1–3 People)
At this stage, there are no strict "departments." Co-founders wear multiple hats. In a standard B2B SaaS startup, responsibilities are usually split down the middle:

### The Builder (Technical / Product)
* **What they do:** Write the code, manage the cloud infrastructure, fix the bugs, and design the database architecture. They set up the GitHub repos, wrestle with deployment pipelines, and ensure the application doesn't crash.
* **The Reality:** They are the entire engineering and design department rolled into one.

### The Seller (Business / CEO)
* **What they do:** Talk to users, close the first 10 contracts, handle the legal paperwork, map out the revenue model, and manually onboard new clients.
* **The Reality:** They are the sales, marketing, and customer support departments.

---

## Phase 2: The Seed Stage (5–15 People)
Once the product is live and generating revenue, the founders raise money (or use profits) to hire specialists. The startup divides into three main pillars:

| Pillar | Key Roles | What They Actually Do |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Engineering & Product** | **Software Engineers** (Frontend, Backend), **Product Manager (PM)**, **Product Designer** | The PM talks to users to figure out what features are missing. The Designer draws the screens. The Engineers write the code to make those screens real. |
| **Go-To-Market (GTM)** | **Account Executives (AE)**, **Marketing** | Marketing gets people to visit the website or book a demo. The AEs do the demos and get the contract signed. |
| **Customer Success (CS)** | **Customer Success Manager (CSM)**, **Support Reps** | Once a sale is closed, the CSM holds the client's hand, migrates their old data, trains their staff, and ensures they are getting value out of the software. |

## How They Actually Work: The Professional Workflow
Startups move fast by using structured workflows, usually a variation of **Agile/Scrum**.

### 1. The Sprints (The Timeframe)
Work is divided into 1-week or 2-week "Sprints." On Monday morning, the team looks at the backlog of ideas and commits to building a specific set of features by Friday.

### 2. The Stand-up (The Daily Sync)
Every morning, the team has a fast, 10-minute meeting. Everyone answers three questions:
* *What did I do yesterday?*
* *What am I doing today?*
* *Am I blocked by anything?*

### 3. The Tooling (The Command Center)
* **Issue Trackers (Linear, Jira):** This is where work lives. Every bug, feature, and task is written down as a "ticket." An engineer claims a ticket, writes the code, and moves the ticket to "Done."
* **Version Control (GitHub):** Engineers do not edit code directly on the live server. They branch the code, make their changes, and open a "Pull Request" (PR). Another engineer reviews the PR.
* **Communication (Slack, Discord):** Urgent issues, deployment alerts, and daily chatter happen here.

## The Product Lifecycle in Action
When a startup decides to build a new feature:
1. **Discovery:** The Founders or Customer Success team notice users complaining about a missing feature.
2. **Scoping:** The Product Manager writes a brief explaining *how* the feature should work.
3. **Design:** The Designer creates visual mockups in Figma.
4. **Development:** Engineers take the mockups and build the frontend UI and backend database logic.
5. **Quality Assurance (QA):** The team tests the feature internally to find bugs.
6. **Deployment:** The code is pushed to production.
7. **Rollout:** Marketing and Customer Success email the users to announce the new feature and explain how to use it.
